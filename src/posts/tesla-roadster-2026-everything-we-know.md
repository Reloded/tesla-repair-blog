The Tesla Roadster has been coming for six years. First promised in 2017 for 2020 delivery. Then pushed to 2022. Then 2023. Then 2024. Then "next year" for two more years. Now there's a real date on the calendar: late April 2026, with the Q1 earnings call on April 22 potentially serving as the launch platform.

Whether you've had a $250,000 deposit tied up since 2017 or you're just watching from the sidelines, here's everything known right now — and why this generation of Roadster is meaningfully different from any production car that exists.

## The Specs That Matter

No car in production comes close to these numbers:

| Spec | Tesla Roadster (Standard) | Tesla Roadster (SpaceX Package) |
|------|--------------------------|--------------------------------|
| 0–60 mph | 1.9 seconds | **1.1 seconds** |
| 0–100 km/h | ~2.1 seconds | ~1.3 seconds |
| Top speed | 400+ km/h (250+ mph) | 400+ km/h |
| Range | 620 miles (998 km) | ~580 miles (estimated) |
| Battery | 200 kWh | 200 kWh |
| Seating | 4 (2+2) | 4 (2+2) |
| Base price | $200,000 | ~$230,000–250,000 (est.) |
| Founders Series | 1,000 units, $250,000 deposit | — |

The 620-mile range with a 200 kWh pack is more than double what any current Tesla offers. For context, the Model S Long Range does about 405 miles on a charge. The Roadster's pack is roughly the size of two Model S batteries, crammed into a low-slung sports car platform.

## The SpaceX Package: How It Actually Works

This is the detail that's generated the most skepticism, so let's be precise about what Tesla is claiming.

The SpaceX Package is **not** rocket propulsion. It uses compressed air — the same principle as a cold-gas reaction control system used on spacecraft for fine attitude adjustments. Here's the hardware:

- **~10 nozzle positions** around the vehicle (front, rear, and underside)
- **Compressed air reservoir** stored in a high-pressure tank (replacing the rear seats in the 2+2 layout, per some leaked images)
- **Electric pump** to pressurize the tank on the fly, driven off the main battery pack
- **Nozzle firing modes:**
  - **Downward nozzles:** create aerodynamic downforce at speed, increase mechanical grip
  - **Forward nozzles:** fire rearward during acceleration (additive thrust)
  - **Rear nozzles:** fire forward during braking (additive braking force)

The 1.1-second 0–60 result comes from firing the forward-facing nozzles at launch simultaneously with maximum motor torque. The downward nozzles keep the front end planted — without them, 1.1-second launches would result in wheelspin and front-end lift.

This is why the SpaceX Package delivers a bigger improvement on 0–60 than on top speed: the thrusters are most effective at low speeds where aerodynamic downforce from the car's body is minimal.

<div class="tip-box">
<strong>How much thrust?</strong> Tesla hasn't published nozzle thrust figures, but leaked testing data suggests the package generates roughly 900–1,000 kg of additional downforce at launch, plus ~150 kg of forward thrust from the rear nozzles. It's a significant force, not a gimmick.
</div>

## How It Compares to the Competition

The Roadster is entering a market that didn't exist when it was first announced. Here's where the production hypercar landscape stands now:

| Car | 0–60 mph | Top Speed | Range | Price |
|-----|----------|-----------|-------|-------|
| **Tesla Roadster (standard)** | 1.9s | 400+ km/h | 620 mi | $200,000 |
| **Tesla Roadster (SpaceX)** | **1.1s** | 400+ km/h | ~580 mi | ~$230–250k (est.) |
| Rimac Nevera | 1.74s | 412 km/h | 340 mi (WLTP) | $2,400,000 |
| Lotus Evija | ~2.9s | 320+ km/h | 250 mi (WLTP) | $2,000,000+ |
| Bugatti Chiron Super Sport | 2.3s | 440 km/h | ~6 mi (irrelevant) | $3,900,000 |
| Porsche 918 Spyder | 2.5s | 345 km/h | N/A (hybrid) | $847,000 |

The Rimac Nevera is the closest like-for-like comparison — an all-electric hypercar built around the same concept. The Roadster undercuts it on price by 12x while beating it on 0–60 by 0.64 seconds (with the SpaceX Package) and more than doubling its range.

## The Delayed History

It's worth being honest about the timeline:

- **November 2017:** Tesla unveils the second-generation Roadster concept alongside the Tesla Semi, promising 2020 delivery
- **2020:** COVID delays cited; pushed to 2021
- **2021:** Pushed to 2022, citing Semi production priority
- **2022:** "Still happening," no date
- **2023:** Focus shifts to Cybertruck; Roadster quietly deferred
- **2024:** Elon states "we'll start production soon," no specifics
- **Early 2026:** Concrete unveil date finally set for late April 2026; engineering sign-off confirmed

The people who put down $50,000 deposits (general reservations) and $250,000 deposits (Founders Series) have been waiting up to nine years. Tesla has maintained those deposits are fully refundable throughout.

## Design: What We Know

The production Roadster will closely resemble the 2017 concept, which showed a genuinely striking low-slung silhouette. Key design details:

- **Convertible glass roof:** removable glass roof panel that stores in the frunk — not a folding hardtop mechanism
- **Yoke steering wheel:** steer-by-wire yoke, not a traditional round wheel (similar to the redesigned Model S/X yoke, but fully drive-by-wire with no mechanical column)
- **2+2 seating:** tight rear seats (enough for children or short trips), front seats are the priority
- **Butterfly doors:** scissor-style doors confirmed in concept; production details not final
- **Camera side mirrors:** digital side mirrors replacing physical glass, similar to current Audi e-tron
- **Track width:** significantly wider than any current Tesla for stability at speed

## Founders Series: What Reservation Holders Should Know

The first 1,000 Rosters off the line are Founders Series units. These were allocated to people who paid $250,000 deposits (full vehicle purchase price) in 2017.

If you're a Founders Series reservation holder:
- Your deposit has been held by Tesla since payment, accruing no interest
- Tesla has confirmed Founders Series will be first to receive production vehicles
- After unveil, expect a formal build configuration window — you'll choose colors, options, and likely whether to add the SpaceX Package
- Delivery timing: 12–18 months post-unveil, so earliest Q3 2027 for front-of-queue Founders Series

General reservation holders (the $50,000 deposit tier) will follow Founders Series. Tesla hasn't confirmed production volume targets for year one but has suggested 10,000–15,000 units annually at full ramp.

## How to Prepare If You're a Reservation Holder

Whether you're in the Founders Series or a general reservation, there are practical steps to take now:

### 1. Verify Your Reservation Status
Log into your Tesla account at tesla.com and confirm your reservation is active. After years of inactivity, some accounts have had issues. Contact Tesla support if anything looks off.

### 2. Update Your Payment Information
Tesla will require a valid payment method on file when the configuration window opens. Outdated cards or billing addresses will delay your order.

### 3. Read Up on Charging Infrastructure
The Roadster's 200 kWh battery will need a V3 Supercharger or better for reasonable charge times. V3 Superchargers deliver up to 250 kW. A full charge from empty at 250 kW would take roughly 48 minutes — but you'll typically charge from 10% to 80%, not 0% to 100%. V4 Superchargers (350 kW) will be more common by delivery time.

Check the Supercharger coverage on your regular routes now. See our [Tesla road trip planning guide](/posts/tesla-road-trip-planning-guide/) for planning long-distance routes.

### 4. Consider Garage Modifications
A 200 kWh car benefits heavily from home charging. If you plan to home-charge, a 48A Level 2 EVSE (11.5 kW) gives you roughly 45 miles of range per hour of charging. With the Roadster's 620-mile range, a full overnight charge from depleted would take 13+ hours — fine for daily use, but worth understanding.

### 5. Arrange Insurance Early
Exotic car insurance for a $200k+ vehicle is a different market than standard auto insurance. Get quotes from specialist insurers well before delivery. Some require agreed-value policies and specific storage requirements.

### 6. Keep Your Current Tesla Updated
If you own a current Tesla, keep it on the latest software. FSD and Autopilot improvements from software updates carry directly to driver familiarity that transfers to the Roadster's more capable platform. See our [Tesla software update guide](/posts/tesla-software-update-stuck/) for troubleshooting update issues.

## The Bottom Line

Six years of delays have understandably eroded trust. But the late April 2026 reveal looks genuine — Tesla has signed off the engineering, the Q1 earnings window creates a natural stage, and the company needs a halo product to counter the ongoing narrative about Model 3/Y aging.

The specs, if delivered as stated, would represent a genuine step change in what a production car can do. The 1.1-second 0–60 with the SpaceX Package isn't a drag-strip-only party trick — it's the result of a fundamentally different approach to vehicle dynamics that combines electric torque with aerodynamic manipulation in a way no production car has done before.

Whether it delivers on time, and at those specs, is a separate question. But for the first time in years, the unveil date feels concrete. Late April is five minutes away. We'll know soon.
