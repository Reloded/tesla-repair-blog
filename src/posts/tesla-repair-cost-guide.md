Tesla service centers are convenient, but they're not cheap. A simple brake pad swap can run $300-500 at the dealer when you can do it yourself for $50-80 in parts. Multiply that across every maintenance item and repair over your ownership period, and DIY saves you thousands.

This guide breaks down what every major Tesla repair costs at the dealer versus doing it yourself, along with difficulty ratings and links to our step-by-step guides.

## How to Use This Guide

- **Dealer Cost** = typical Tesla Service Center or third-party shop price (parts + labor)
- **DIY Cost** = parts only, sourced online
- **Difficulty** = Easy (no experience needed), Intermediate (basic tools + some confidence), Advanced (significant mechanical skill)
- All prices are approximate and vary by region and model

## Before You Start

1. **Get the right tools.** A basic toolkit, floor jack, and jack stands cover 80% of the repairs on this page. See our tool recommendations at the top.
2. **Know your model year.** Parts and procedures vary between Model 3/Y and Model S/X, and even between production years.
3. **Put the car in Jack Mode.** Always enable Jack Mode in the service menu before lifting your Tesla. Our [lifting guide](/posts/tesla-jack-points-lifting-guide/) covers this.
4. **Start easy.** If you've never worked on a car, begin with cabin filter, tire rotation, or wiper blades. Build confidence before tackling brakes or suspension.
5. **Document everything.** Take photos before and during disassembly. Your future self will thank you.
