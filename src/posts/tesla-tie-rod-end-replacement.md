Worn tie rod ends are one of the most common steering issues on Tesla Model 3 and Model Y. They cause loose steering feel, uneven tire wear, and sometimes a noticeable clunking when turning. The good news: outer tie rod ends are one of the simpler suspension repairs you can do yourself.

![Tesla front suspension showing tie rod end connection to steering knuckle](/images/repairs/tesla-tie-rod-end-steering.jpg)
*The outer tie rod end connects the steering rack to the steering knuckle. When it wears out, you lose precise steering control.*

## Symptoms of Worn Tie Rod Ends

**While Driving:**
- Loose or vague steering feel
- Steering wheel doesn't return to center smoothly
- Vibration at highway speeds
- Car wanders or drifts despite proper alignment
- Squealing sound when turning

**Visual Signs:**
- Uneven tire wear (inside or outside edge)
- Torn or cracked tie rod boot
- Visible play when grabbing the tire at 3 and 9 o'clock and rocking it

**The Shake Test:**
Lift the front of the car and grab the tire at 3 and 9 o'clock (horizontal). Push and pull. Any clicking or play means your tie rod end or ball joint is worn. To isolate the tie rod, have someone watch the tie rod joint while you rock the wheel.

<div class="tip-box">
<strong>Inner vs. Outer:</strong> Outer tie rod ends fail far more often than inner tie rod ends. The outer end has a ball joint exposed to road spray and debris, while the inner is protected by the steering rack boot. This guide covers outer tie rod end replacement â€” the repair you'll most likely need.
</div>

## Why Tie Rod Ends Fail

1. **Road salt and water** â€” Ball joint boot degrades, water enters
2. **Potholes and curb hits** â€” Impact loading accelerates wear
3. **Mileage** â€” Normal wear item, typically 80,000â€“150,000 km
4. **Boot damage** â€” Once the boot tears, the joint is on borrowed time

## DIY Replacement Guide

### Tools Required

- Floor jack (3+ ton)
- Jack stands
- 22mm deep socket (castle nut)
- 21mm combination wrench (jam nut)
- 14mm combination wrench (hold tie rod)
- Ball joint separator (fork type or screw type)
- Torque wrench (capable of 80 Nm and 180 Nm)
- White paint marker or correction fluid
- Penetrating oil (PB Blaster)
- Needle-nose pliers (for cotter pin)
- 10mm 12-point combination wrench (to hold stud from spinning)

### Step 1: Preparation

1. Park on level, solid ground
2. Loosen front lug nuts slightly (don't remove yet)
3. Lift the front with floor jack at the [proper jack point](/posts/tesla-jack-points-lifting-guide/)
4. Secure on jack stands
5. Remove the front wheel

### Step 2: Mark the Position

This is the most important step for getting your alignment close enough to drive to the shop.

1. Use a paint marker to make alignment marks on the tie rod and jam nut
2. Count and record the number of exposed threads between the jam nut and tie rod end
3. Take a photo for reference

<div class="tip-box">
<strong>Why this matters:</strong> The position of the tie rod end on the tie rod controls your toe angle. Getting it back to the same position means your alignment will be close enough to drive safely to the alignment shop. Off by even a few turns and your tires will scrub badly.
</div>

### Step 3: Remove the Old Tie Rod End

1. Spray the castle nut and jam nut with penetrating oil â€” wait 10 minutes
2. Remove the cotter pin from the castle nut using needle-nose pliers
3. Remove the castle nut (22mm) from the tie rod end stud
   - Use a 10mm wrench on the stud flat to prevent spinning if needed
4. Use a ball joint separator to pop the tie rod end out of the steering knuckle
   - Position the separator between the knuckle and the tie rod end
   - Tighten until the taper breaks free with a pop

<div class="warning-box">
<strong>Do NOT hammer the stud directly.</strong> This damages the steering knuckle bore. Always use a proper ball joint separator.
</div>

5. Loosen the jam nut (21mm) â€” you'll need two wrenches: 21mm on the jam nut and 14mm to hold the tie rod
6. Unscrew the tie rod end from the tie rod, counting the turns as you go
7. Record the number of turns for installation

### Step 4: Install the New Tie Rod End

1. Thread the new tie rod end onto the tie rod the exact same number of turns
2. Align it to your paint marks
3. Insert the stud into the steering knuckle
4. Hand-thread the castle nut

### Step 5: Torque to Specification

**Torque Values (Model 3/Model Y) â€” verified from [Tesla Service Manual](https://service.tesla.com):**
- **Tie rod end nut (to knuckle):** 180 Nm (132.7 ft-lb) â€” use a **new** nut (Tesla P/N 1109263-00-B)
- **Jam nut (outer tie rod to tie rod):** 80 Nm (59.0 ft-lb)

**Tightening Procedure:**
1. Torque the castle nut to spec
2. If the cotter pin hole doesn't align, tighten further (never loosen to align) â€” do not exceed the next hole alignment
3. Install new cotter pin and bend the ends
4. Torque the jam nut against the tie rod
5. Double-check that the tie rod end is positioned at your reference marks

### Step 6: Reassemble

1. Reinstall the wheel
2. Hand-tighten lug nuts in a star pattern
3. Lower the car off jack stands
4. Torque lug nuts to 175 Nm (129 ft-lb)

### Step 7: Repeat for the Other Side

If replacing both sides (recommended), mirror the procedure.

## Post-Installation

### Wheel Alignment â€” Mandatory

**Do not skip this.** Even if you matched the marks perfectly, the new tie rod end may have slightly different geometry.

- Drive carefully and directly to the alignment shop (under 15 km)
- Expect to pay $80â€“150
- Request a printout so you can verify specs
- Check alignment again after 500 km to confirm nothing shifted

### Test Drive Checklist

After alignment:
- [ ] Steering wheel centered while driving straight
- [ ] No play or looseness in steering
- [ ] No clunks when turning
- [ ] Car tracks straight on flat road
- [ ] No vibration at highway speed

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Not marking the position** â€” You'll have no alignment reference and risk destroying tires on the drive to the shop
2. **Hammering the stud** â€” Damages the knuckle. Use a separator.
3. **Reusing the cotter pin** â€” Always use a new one. They're designed for single use.
4. **Skipping alignment** â€” Even "perfect" mark matching needs verification
5. **Only replacing one side** â€” The other side is likely almost as worn
6. **Forgetting to tighten the jam nut** â€” The tie rod end will rotate and your alignment will drift dangerously

## When to Call a Professional

- If the castle nut or jam nut is severely corroded and won't budge
- If you find play in the inner tie rod too (requires special tools)
- If the steering knuckle bore is damaged
- If you're not comfortable working under the car
