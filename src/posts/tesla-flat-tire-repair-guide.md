You're driving your Tesla and the tire pressure warning flashes on screen. You pull over, check the tire, and it's flat. Now what? Unlike most cars, your Tesla doesn't have a spare tire in the trunk. No jack, no lug wrench, no donut spare — nothing.

Don't panic. You have several solid options, and most flat tires can be fixed roadside in under 30 minutes. This guide covers every scenario — from plugging it yourself to calling for a tow — with real costs and step-by-step instructions.

## Why Teslas Don't Have Spare Tires

No Tesla — Model 3, Y, S, or X — comes with a spare tire. There are three reasons:

1. **Weight reduction.** A spare tire, jack, and tools add 40-50 lbs. Less weight means more range.
2. **Trunk space.** The sub-trunk (frunk on S/X) is designed for cargo, not a spare. Tesla prioritized usable storage.
3. **Modern tire design.** Tesla uses acoustic foam-lined tires that reduce road noise. They're specialized — a generic spare wouldn't match anyway.

Some Model S and X vehicles (pre-2021) shipped with a tire repair kit (sealant canister + 12V inflator). Newer Model 3 and Y vehicles may include one, but many don't — check your sub-trunk. If you don't have one, it's worth buying a kit or plug set to keep in the car.

## What to Do When You Get a Flat

Follow this procedure every time:

1. **Pull over safely.** Get off the road completely. Use the shoulder, a parking lot, or any flat surface away from traffic.
2. **Turn on hazard lights.** Tap the hazard button on the touchscreen or press the physical button (Model 3/Y: top of the screen; Model S/X: overhead console).
3. **Put the car in Park.** The car won't roll — EVs hold position without a parking brake engaged, but engage it anyway.
4. **Assess the damage.** Get out and look at the tire. Check for:
   - **Nail or screw in the tread** → repairable (plug or sealant)
   - **Sidewall damage (bulge, cut, gash)** → NOT repairable, needs replacement
   - **Tire completely shredded** → needs replacement and possibly a new wheel

<div class="warning-box">

⚠️ **Never drive on a flat tire.** Even 100 yards will destroy the wheel rim ($300-800 to replace) and can damage suspension components. If the tire is flat, stop immediately.

</div>

5. **Decide your fix:** If it's a small tread puncture, try Option 1 or 2 below. If the sidewall is damaged or the tire is shredded, skip to Option 3 or 4.

## Option 1: Use Tesla's Tire Repair Kit

If your Tesla came with the tire repair kit (check the sub-trunk), here's how to use it:

**What's in the kit:** A canister of liquid tire sealant and a small 12V air compressor that plugs into the car's 12V outlet.

**Step by step:**

1. Remove the kit from the sub-trunk and unpack it.
2. Shake the sealant canister well (30 seconds).
3. Remove the valve cap from the flat tire.
4. Attach the sealant canister hose to the tire valve stem.
5. Connect the compressor to the sealant canister (the sealant feeds through the compressor).
6. Plug the compressor into the car's 12V outlet (center console or trunk area).
7. Turn on the compressor. It will push sealant into the tire and then inflate it.
8. Inflate to the recommended PSI: **42 PSI for Model 3/Y**, **45 PSI for Model S/X** (check the door jamb sticker for your exact spec).
9. Drive slowly (under 40 mph) for 5-10 minutes to spread the sealant evenly inside the tire.
10. Stop and recheck the pressure. Top off if needed.

<div class="info-box">

ℹ️ **Limitations:** The sealant only works on small punctures in the tread (under 4mm diameter). It will NOT fix sidewall damage, large gashes, or bead leaks. It's a temporary fix — get the tire properly repaired or replaced within 100 miles. Also, sealant makes a mess inside the tire, which may cost extra to clean when you get a permanent repair ($20-40 cleaning fee at most shops).

</div>

**Tip:** Check your sealant's expiration date. Most canisters expire after 4-6 years. Replace it if it's expired — dried-out sealant won't work.

## Option 2: Plug the Tire Yourself

A tire plug kit is more reliable than sealant and gives you a longer-lasting repair. Many Tesla owners keep one in their trunk permanently.

**What you need:** A mushroom plug kit (also called a tire plug kit) — includes plug strips, a reamer tool, and an insertion tool. Plus a portable 12V air compressor.

**Step by step:**

1. **Locate the puncture.** If you can see a nail or screw, mark its position. If not, spray soapy water on the tread — bubbles will form at the leak.
2. **Remove the object** (nail, screw) with pliers. Keep track of the hole.
3. **Ream the hole.** Insert the reamer tool into the puncture and push it in and out 3-4 times. This cleans and slightly enlarges the hole so the plug fits.
4. **Thread the plug.** Push a plug strip through the eye of the insertion tool so it's centered.
5. **Insert the plug.** Push the insertion tool with the plug into the hole firmly until about ½ inch of plug sticks out.
6. **Pull the tool straight out.** The plug stays in the tire. Trim the excess flush with the tread using a razor blade.
7. **Inflate the tire** to spec (42 PSI for Model 3/Y, 45 PSI for Model S/X) using your 12V compressor.
8. **Check for leaks** with soapy water. No bubbles = good seal.

<div class="info-box">

ℹ️ **When plugging works:** Tread punctures from nails, screws, or small debris up to about 6mm (¼ inch). The puncture must be in the center portion of the tread — not within 1 inch of the sidewall.

**When it doesn't:** Sidewall punctures, cuts longer than ¼ inch, bead damage, or multiple punctures close together. In these cases, the tire needs replacement.

</div>

A properly installed tire plug can last the remaining life of the tire. Many shops will do a combination plug-patch (from the inside) for a more permanent repair — that's the gold standard if you can get to a shop.

For more on maintaining proper tire pressure after a repair, see our [Tesla tire pressure guide](/posts/tesla-tire-pressure-guide/).

## Option 3: Call Tesla Roadside Assistance

If you can't fix it roadside — or you'd rather not — Tesla Roadside Assistance will come to you.

**How to call:**
- **Tesla app:** Open the app → tap "Roadside Assistance" → follow the prompts
- **Phone:** Call **1-877-798-3752** (available 24/7)

**What they do:** Tesla sends a **flatbed tow truck**. This is important — Teslas cannot be flat-towed (dragged with wheels on the ground) because it damages the electric motors. Always insist on a flatbed if a non-Tesla tow service arrives.

**Cost:**
- **Within warranty (4 years / 50,000 miles):** Free towing up to 500 miles
- **Within Roadside coverage (if purchased):** Free towing, limits vary
- **Out of warranty/coverage:** You pay the tow truck company directly. Typical cost: $75-150 for local towing

<div class="warning-box">

⚠️ **Never let anyone flat-tow your Tesla.** All Tesla models are AWD or have permanent magnet motors. Dragging the wheels generates electricity with nowhere to go, which can fry the drive unit. Flatbed only — no exceptions.

</div>

**Response time:** Typically 30-90 minutes depending on your location. Rural areas may take longer.

## Option 4: Mobile Tire Service

Several companies offer mobile tire replacement — they come to your location with a new tire and swap it on the spot.

**Popular options:**
- **TreadConnect** — Tesla-focused mobile tire service
- **Treads** — operates in many US cities
- **Your local tire shop** — many now offer mobile service

**Typical cost:** $75-150 for the service call, plus the cost of the tire itself. Total usually runs $250-500 depending on the tire size.

**When to use this:** When you have sidewall damage or a blowout and can't plug the tire. It saves you the hassle of a tow — they bring the tire to you.

## Should You Buy a Compact Spare?

Aftermarket compact spare kits are available for Tesla Model 3 and Model Y. They fit in the sub-trunk and include a small spare tire, a jack, and a lug wrench.

**Pros:**
- True peace of mind — works for any flat, including sidewall blowouts
- Fits in the sub-trunk without losing much cargo space
- Lets you drive to a tire shop at up to 50 mph
- One-time purchase: $200-400

**Cons:**
- Adds 30-40 lbs of weight (minor range impact, ~2-5 miles)
- Takes up sub-trunk space
- You still need to swap the tire manually (15-20 minutes)
- Not available for Model S/X (different bolt patterns and sizes — fewer aftermarket options)

<div class="info-box">

ℹ️ **If you do a lot of highway driving or road trips,** a compact spare is worth it. If you mostly drive in the city near tire shops, a plug kit and compressor are usually enough.

</div>

When using a jack on your Tesla, always use proper jack pads on the designated lift points to avoid crushing the battery pack. Check our [tire rotation guide](/posts/tesla-tire-rotation/) for lift point locations.

## Prevention Tips

The best flat tire is the one that never happens. Here's how to reduce your risk:

1. **Monitor tire pressure weekly.** Your Tesla shows tire pressures on the touchscreen (Controls → Service → Tire Pressure or swipe left on the main screen). Keep them at spec — underinflated tires are more prone to damage. See our [tire pressure guide](/posts/tesla-tire-pressure-guide/) for details.

2. **Check tires after construction zones.** Nails and screws are the #1 cause of flats. If you drove through a construction area, do a quick visual check at your next stop.

3. **Inspect tread depth regularly.** Worn tires are more vulnerable to punctures. Tesla tires wear faster than average due to the instant torque — [rotate them every 6,250 miles](/posts/tesla-tire-rotation/) to extend their life.

4. **Avoid curbs.** Sidewall damage from curb strikes can cause slow leaks or blowouts later. This is especially common with the low-profile tires on 20"+ wheels (Model S Plaid, Model X).

5. **Keep a repair kit in the car.** At minimum: a plug kit and a 12V compressor. Total cost: under $40. It sits in the sub-trunk and you'll forget it's there until you need it.

6. **Replace TPMS sensors when needed.** If your tire pressure monitoring system isn't reading correctly, you might miss a slow leak. Check our [TPMS sensor replacement guide](/posts/tesla-tpms-sensor-replacement/) if you're getting sensor warnings.

<div class="info-box">

ℹ️ **Quick kit recommendation:** At minimum, keep a tire plug kit ($10-15) and a portable 12V compressor ($30-50) in your sub-trunk. This combination handles 90% of roadside flats and takes up almost no space.

</div>

A flat tire on a Tesla is an inconvenience, not a disaster. With a $25 plug kit in your trunk, you can fix most flats yourself in 15 minutes and be back on the road. For everything else, Tesla Roadside Assistance and mobile tire services have you covered.
