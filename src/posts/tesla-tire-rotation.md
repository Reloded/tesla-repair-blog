## Why Tesla Tires Wear Faster

Several factors contribute to faster tire wear on Teslas:

- **Instant torque** - Electric motors deliver full torque immediately, causing more tire slip
- **Heavy vehicle weight** - Battery packs add significant weight
- **Performance tires** - Many Teslas come with soft compound tires for grip
- **Aggressive traction control** - Constant micro-adjustments wear tires
- **Rear-wheel bias** - RWD and AWD models put more stress on rear tires

<div class="info-box">
<strong>Typical Tesla Tire Lifespan:</strong> 25,000-40,000 miles depending on driving style, tire compound, and rotation frequency. Without rotations, rear tires may wear out in 15,000-20,000 miles.
</div>

## Rotation Schedule

**Recommended interval:** Every 6,250 miles (or at each tire shop visit)

Tesla recommends rotating tires at every service interval, which is roughly 12,500 miles. However, due to faster wear, many owners rotate every 5,000-7,500 miles for best results.

## Rotation Patterns by Drivetrain

The rotation pattern depends on your Tesla's drivetrain configuration:

### Rear-Wheel Drive (RWD)

Pattern: **Rearward Cross** or **X-Pattern**

- Front right â†’ Rear left
- Rear left â†’ Front left
- Rear right â†’ Front right

This puts the less-worn fronts on the harder-working rear axle.

### All-Wheel Drive (AWD)

Pattern: **X-Pattern** (most common)
- Front left â†’ Rear right
- Front right â†’ Rear left
- Rear left â†’ Front right
- Rear right â†’ Front left

### Staggered Wheels (Different Front/Rear Sizes)

If your Tesla has different sized wheels front and rear (common on Performance models):

**You cannot rotate front-to-rear.** Only rotate:
- Front left â†” Front right
- Rear left â†” Rear right

This is called a **side-to-side rotation**. It's less effective but still helps even out wear.

<div class="warning-box">
<strong>Check Your Tires:</strong> Before rotating, verify if your tires are directional (they can only rotate one way) or asymmetric. Directional tires can only swap side-to-side on the same axle.

## Step-by-Step DIY Rotation

### Preparation

1. Park on a flat, level surface
2. Enable **Jack Mode**: Controls > Service > Jack Mode
3. Gather all tools
4. If checking tire pressure, do it before starting (cold tires)

### The Rotation Process

**Step 1: Break Lug Nuts Loose**

While the car is still on the ground with weight on the tires:
- Use the breaker bar with 21mm socket
- Loosen each lug nut 1/4 to 1/2 turn
- Do this for all four wheels before lifting

**Step 2: Lift the Vehicle**

You have two options:

**Option A - One Corner at a Time:**
1. Lift one corner
2. Place on jack stand
3. Remove wheel
4. Move to the corner it's going to
5. Repeat for each wheel

**Option B - All Four Corners (Faster):**
1. Lift and support all four corners on jack stands
2. Remove all wheels
3. Swap according to rotation pattern
4. This requires 4 jack stands

<div class="tip-box">
<strong>Jack Puck Required:</strong> Always use a Tesla-specific jack puck or pad when lifting. The battery pack is exposed and can be damaged by a standard jack.
</div>

**Step 3: Reinstall Wheels**

1. Hand-thread lug nuts to prevent cross-threading
2. Snug them down with the socket
3. Lower the car partially (wheel touching ground with some weight)
4. Torque in a **star pattern** to **129 ft-lbs** (Model 3/Y) or **175 ft-lbs** (Model S/X - check your manual)
5. Lower fully and remove jack

**Step 4: Reset TPMS (If Needed)**

Tesla's TPMS usually auto-calibrates after a few miles of driving. If you get a tire pressure warning:

1. Go to Controls > Service > Reset TPMS
2. Drive for 10-15 minutes at varying speeds
3. Sensors should recalibrate automatically

## Post-Rotation Checklist

- [ ] All lug nuts torqued to spec
- [ ] Jack mode disabled
- [ ] Tire pressures correct (check after driving)
- [ ] No TPMS warnings (or reset in progress)
- [ ] Test drive for any vibrations

## Additional Tips

### Checking Tire Wear

While the tires are off, check tread depth:
- New tires: 10/32" to 11/32"
- Replace at: 4/32" (wet weather) or 2/32" (legal minimum)
- Use a tread depth gauge or the penny test

### Common Wear Patterns and Causes

| Pattern | Cause | Fix |
|---------|-------|-----|
| Center wear | Over-inflation | Reduce pressure |
| Edge wear | Under-inflation | Increase pressure |
| One-sided wear | Alignment issue | Get alignment |
| Cupping/scalloping | Suspension problem | Inspect suspension |

### When to Skip DIY

Consider professional service if:
- You notice alignment issues (pulling, uneven wear)
- Wheels need balancing (vibration at highway speeds)
- You're uncomfortable with the process
- You need tires dismounted and remounted
