Scratches are the number one cosmetic complaint among Tesla owners — and it's not your imagination. Tesla's paint really is softer and thinner than most cars on the road. The good news: about 80% of scratches you'll encounter are surface-level clear coat marks that you can fix at home for under $50 in materials.

Before you book a $500+ appointment at a body shop, read this guide. We'll walk through how to identify your scratch type, match your Tesla's paint code, and fix it yourself — step by step.

## Types of Scratches: The Fingernail Test

Not all scratches are the same. Run your fingernail across the scratch perpendicular to its direction. What you feel tells you exactly what you're dealing with:

**Level 1 — Clear Coat Scratch (most common)**
Your fingernail slides right over it. You can see the scratch but can't feel it. This is only in the clear coat layer — the paint underneath is untouched. Shopping cart marks, car wash swirls, and light key brushes fall here. **Fix: compound and polish.**

**Level 2 — Paint Scratch**
Your fingernail catches slightly. The scratch has cut through the clear coat into the color layer, but you still see color (not gray or white). **Fix: touch-up paint + polish.**

**Level 3 — Primer Scratch**
Your fingernail definitely catches, and you can see white or gray underneath. The scratch has gone through the color coat down to the primer. **Fix: primer + color coat + clear coat touch-up.**

**Level 4 — Deep Scratch (bare metal)**
You see shiny metal or the scratch is wide enough to fit a fingernail into. This needs professional repair — attempting DIY on deep scratches usually makes them worse. **Fix: body shop.**

<div class="info-box">

**Quick rule:** If you can't feel it with your fingernail, you can almost certainly buff it out yourself. That covers the majority of Tesla scratches.

</div>

## How to Fix Clear Coat Scratches (Level 1)

This is the most common repair you'll do. Total cost: **$15–40 in supplies**.

**What you need:**
- Car wash soap and microfiber wash mitt
- Isopropyl alcohol (IPA) spray or panel wipe
- Cutting compound (Meguiar's Ultimate Compound works well)
- Finishing polish (Meguiar's ScratchX 2.0 or Ultimate Polish)
- Microfiber polishing pads (or a dual-action polisher for larger areas)
- Carnauba wax or spray sealant

**Step-by-step:**

1. **Wash the area** thoroughly with car wash soap. Dirt particles during polishing create new scratches. Dry completely.

2. **Clean with IPA** — spray isopropyl alcohol on the scratch area and wipe with a clean microfiber. This removes wax and contaminants so you can see the true scratch depth.

3. **Apply compound** — put a small amount of cutting compound on a microfiber pad or foam applicator. Work it into the scratch using firm, back-and-forth motions along the scratch direction (not circles). Spend 30–60 seconds per area.

4. **Wipe and inspect** — clean off the compound with a microfiber cloth. The scratch should be significantly reduced or gone. Repeat if needed — two passes usually does it.

5. **Polish** — switch to a finishing polish on a clean pad. This removes the micro-haze left by the compound and restores the gloss. Light pressure, 30 seconds per area.

6. **Protect** — apply wax or sealant to protect your fresh work. This also blends the repaired area with the surrounding paint.

<div class="warning-box">

**Don't overdo it.** Tesla's clear coat is thinner than average. Two or three compound passes max. If the scratch isn't gone after that, it's deeper than Level 1 — move to the Level 2 method below.

</div>

**Dual-action polisher vs. hand polishing:** A DA polisher ($30–80) makes this faster and more consistent, especially for swirl marks across a whole panel. For a single scratch, hand polishing works fine. If you plan to do any [paint chip repair](/posts/tesla-paint-chip-repair/) or regular detailing, a DA polisher is worth the investment.

## How to Fix Paint Scratches (Level 2)

When the scratch cuts into the color coat, you need to put paint back before polishing. Total cost: **$25–50**.

**What you need:**
- Everything from Level 1, plus:
- Tesla OEM touch-up paint (matched to your paint code)
- Fine-tip applicator brush or toothpick
- 2000-grit wet sandpaper
- Spray bottle with water

**Step-by-step:**

1. **Clean the scratch** — wash, dry, and wipe with IPA just like Level 1. The scratch must be free of wax, dirt, and debris.

2. **Apply touch-up paint** — use a fine-tip brush or toothpick to carefully fill only the scratch with paint. Apply thin layers. You want the paint slightly above the surface — it will shrink as it cures. Let each layer dry 15–20 minutes.

3. **Let it cure** — wait at least 24–48 hours for the touch-up paint to fully harden. Rushing this step ruins the repair.

4. **Wet sand** — soak 2000-grit sandpaper in water for 10 minutes. Wrap it around a small sanding block or eraser. Using light pressure with plenty of water, sand the touch-up paint flush with the surrounding surface. Keep the sandpaper wet at all times.

5. **Compound and polish** — follow Steps 3–6 from the Level 1 method above. The compound removes sanding haze, the polish restores gloss.

**Dr. ColorChip kits** are an easier alternative for Level 2 scratches. They use a paint-and-blending-solution system that skips the wet sanding step. Results aren't as invisible as the full method, but they're faster and more forgiving for beginners. Also great for [paint chip repair](/posts/tesla-paint-chip-repair/).

## How to Fix Deeper Scratches (Level 3)

Primer-depth scratches require a multi-layer approach. This is the most advanced DIY repair — take your time. Total cost: **$30–60**.

**What you need:**
- Everything from Level 2, plus:
- Automotive primer (spray can or brush-on)
- Automotive clear coat (spray can or brush-on)
- 1500-grit and 2000-grit wet sandpaper
- Masking tape and paper/plastic sheeting

1. **Clean and prep** — wash, dry, IPA wipe. If there's any rust in the scratch, lightly sand it out with 1500-grit and apply a rust converter before proceeding.

2. **Mask the area** — tape off the surrounding paint, leaving only the scratch exposed. This prevents overspray on good paint.

3. **Apply primer** — thin coat of automotive primer into the scratch. Let it dry 30 minutes. Lightly sand flush with 2000-grit wet sandpaper.

4. **Apply color coat** — use your matched Tesla touch-up paint. Two thin coats, 15 minutes between each. Let cure overnight.

5. **Apply clear coat** — one or two thin coats of automotive clear coat over the color. Let cure 24 hours.

6. **Wet sand and polish** — 2000-grit wet sand to level, then compound and polish per the Level 1 method.

<div class="warning-box">

**Level 3 repairs are visible up close** on metallic and multi-coat Tesla colors (Pearl White, Ultra Red, Quicksilver). The metallic flakes and multi-layer pearl effects are nearly impossible to replicate with touch-up paint. The repair will prevent rust and look fine from a few feet away, but it won't be factory-perfect.

</div>

## When to Go to a Professional

Some damage is beyond DIY. Take it to a professional if:

- **Bare metal is exposed** (Level 4) — these need proper body shop prep, primer, base coat, and clear coat with a spray gun
- **The scratch spans multiple panels** — door-to-fender scratches need professional blending
- **Deep bumper scuffs** — plastic bumpers require flexible paint and specific prep
- **You want invisible results on metallic colors** — a professional can spray-blend to match metallic flakes
- **Insurance is covering it** — if someone hit your car, let the body shop handle it and bill their insurance

For body shop work, get quotes from Tesla-certified shops. Non-certified shops sometimes struggle with Tesla's paint formulations and the aluminum body panels on Model S/X.

## Why Tesla Paint Scratches So Easily

It's not a defect — it's a trade-off. Here's what's going on:

**Water-based paint.** Tesla uses water-based paint at their factories, which is better for the environment but produces a softer finish than traditional solvent-based automotive paint.

**Thinner clear coat.** Tesla's clear coat layer measures around 1.5–2.0 mils compared to 2.0–3.0 mils on most German cars. Less material means less room for error and less protection.

**Softer overall.** Paint hardness tests consistently show Tesla paint scoring lower than BMW, Mercedes, or Audi. This means scratches show up from contact that wouldn't mark a harder finish.

**Darker colors show it more.** Black and Midnight Silver show every swirl and scratch because of contrast. Pearl White hides minor blemishes better thanks to the multi-coat pearl layer.

This is one reason so many Tesla owners invest in [PPF (paint protection film)](/posts/tesla-ppf-installation/) right after purchase. Prevention beats repair every time.

## Prevention: Stop Scratches Before They Happen

**Paint Protection Film (PPF)** — the gold standard. A clear urethane film applied over the paint that absorbs scratches and rock chips. Full front coverage runs $1,500–2,500 but pays for itself in prevented damage. See our [full PPF installation guide](/posts/tesla-ppf-installation/).

**Ceramic coating** — won't prevent scratches but adds a hard, slick layer that reduces swirl marks from washing and makes the paint easier to maintain. $50–100 DIY, $500–1,500 professional. Read our [ceramic coating guide](/posts/tesla-ceramic-coating/).

**Touchless car washes** — automatic car washes with spinning brushes are the #1 cause of swirl marks. Use touchless washes or hand wash only.

**Parking habits** — park far from other cars when possible. End spots and pull-through spots reduce door ding risk. If you have a garage, use it.

**Microfiber everything** — never use paper towels, old t-shirts, or chamois on Tesla paint. Microfiber wash mitts and drying towels only. Keep them clean — a dirty microfiber is just sandpaper.
