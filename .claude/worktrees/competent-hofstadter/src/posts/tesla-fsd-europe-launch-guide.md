April 10, 2026 is shaping up to be a big date for European Tesla owners. That's when the Dutch RDW (Netherlands Vehicle Authority) is expected to grant type approval for Tesla FSD (Supervised) v14 — the regulatory green light that kicks off the EU-wide rollout.

If you've been on Basic Autopilot while watching US Tesla owners navigate city streets hands-free, the wait is almost over. Here's everything you need to know right now, and exactly how to prepare your car.

## The April 10 Date: What's Actually Happening

The Netherlands RDW is the EU's go-to body for Tesla type approvals — they previously approved the Model 3 and Model Y for EU-wide sale. Tesla filed for FSD approval through the RDW because:

1. The RDW has an established working relationship with Tesla
2. EU mutual recognition rules mean one approval unlocks the whole bloc
3. The Netherlands has been accommodating of Tesla's testing requests

The original target was March 20, 2026. RDW pushed it back to April 10 to complete additional testing of FSD v14's roundabout handling and intersection behavior on Dutch roads. That delay is actually a good sign — it means regulators are taking the validation seriously rather than rubber-stamping it.

**What approval means in practice:**
- Tesla can push FSD (Supervised) OTA to eligible cars in the Netherlands immediately
- Other EU member states can recognize the approval under **Article 39 of Regulation (EU) 2018/858** without running their own full type approval process
- Germany, France, and Norway are expected to follow within weeks
- Broader EU rollout through summer 2026

<div class="tip-box">
<strong>UK note:</strong> The UK is no longer under EU type approval — it follows UNECE regulations via the DVSA. Tesla is pursuing a parallel approval there, expected in a similar timeframe but on a separate track.
</div>

## What Is FSD v14?

FSD (Supervised) v14 is a significant generation jump from what North American users launched with. Key improvements relevant to European driving:

| Feature | FSD v12/v13 | FSD v14 |
|---------|------------|---------|
| Roundabout handling | Inconsistent | Dedicated training set (4,500+ scenarios) |
| Cyclist prediction | Basic | Multi-agent behavior modeling |
| Narrow street handling | Poor | Improved via EU testing data |
| Low-light performance | Camera-dependent | Enhanced neural net processing |
| Lane change confirmation | Auto in US | Expected: driver-confirm tap (EU regulation) |
| Training data | US-heavy | 1.6M km EU roads added |

The "Supervised" designation is important and won't change at launch. You must keep your hands on the wheel and eyes on the road. The car monitors this via torque sensors in the steering wheel and the cabin camera that watches your face. Nod off or look away for too long — it disengages and pulls over.

### What FSD v14 Does in Practice

- Navigates from highway on-ramp to your driveway with minimal intervention
- Handles traffic lights, stop signs, roundabouts, and unprotected turns
- Signals and changes lanes based on your navigation route
- Adjusts speed for school zones, speed bumps, curves
- Detects pedestrians, cyclists, and scooters and yields appropriately
- Parks itself in a space when you arrive

What it does **not** do: operate without a driver present. This is not a robotaxi.

## The Evidence: EU Testing Results

Tesla hasn't been sitting still while regulators deliberated. The EU testing data is substantial:

- **1.6 million km** logged on European roads in testing
- **13,000+ customer ride-alongs** in France, Italy, and Germany in late 2025
- **4,500+ distinct track scenarios** covering European road situations
- Software update **2026.8.6** (released March 2026) includes FSD v14 hooks that are region-locked but visible in the code

The demo rides in France, Italy, and Germany got strong reviews from automotive press. The consistent feedback: roundabout handling was noticeably better than expected, and the car was more conservative on narrow European streets than US behavior — which is appropriate given the different road environments.

## Hardware: Is Your Tesla Eligible?

FSD requires a compatible Full Self-Driving Computer. Here's the breakdown:

| Hardware | FSD v14 Eligible? | Typical Vehicles |
|----------|------------------|--------------------|
| HW2.5 | ❌ Needs upgrade | Pre-2019 vehicles |
| HW3 (FSD Computer) | ✅ Yes (second wave) | 2019–2023 models |
| HW4 (AI4) | ✅ Yes (first wave) | 2024+ models (Highland/Juniper) |

HW4 owners will likely get FSD first — Tesla traditionally rolls new regions to newest hardware before backporting. HW3 should follow within weeks to months, not years.

**How to check your hardware version:**

1. Tap **Controls** on the touchscreen
2. Tap **Software**
3. Tap **Additional Vehicle Information**
4. Look for "Full Self-Driving Computer" — it'll show HW3 or HW4

If you see HW2.5, contact a Tesla Service Center about a computer upgrade. Cost is approximately €1,500 and requires a Service Center visit — not a DIY job. Book early, as demand will spike after approval.

All current Tesla models — S, X, 3, Y — with HW3 or HW4 are eligible. No model is excluded.

## Pricing: What to Expect

Tesla ended the one-time FSD purchase option globally on February 14, 2026 (US price was $8,000). Europe never had the one-time purchase, so this doesn't affect EU owners. When FSD launches here, it will be subscription-only.

**Expected European pricing:**

| Subscription | Estimated Price | Who Qualifies |
|-------------|----------------|---------------|
| FSD (Supervised) | €99–149/month | All eligible vehicles |
| FSD with Enhanced Autopilot | €49–79/month | Vehicles with EAP package |

These are estimates based on US pricing ($99/month, $49/month with EAP) plus EU VAT adjustment. Tesla hasn't confirmed European prices yet. Expect the official announcement the same week as RDW approval.

The subscription model is actually a low-risk way to try it. Sign up for one month, see how it handles your daily commute, cancel if it doesn't suit you. No €8,000 commitment required.

## How to Prepare Your Tesla for FSD Right Now

This is the practical part. Do these things before April 10 and you'll be ready to enable FSD the day it arrives.

### Step 1: Verify Your Hardware

As above — Controls > Software > Additional Vehicle Information. Screenshot it so you have it handy.

### Step 2: Run a Full Software Update

FSD v14 will arrive as an OTA update, but only if your vehicle is on a recent enough base firmware. Check and install any pending updates now:

1. Tap **Controls > Software**
2. If an update is available, schedule it for overnight or when plugged in
3. After installing, confirm you're on the latest version

Keep your car connected to Wi-Fi overnight — Tesla pushes large updates in off-peak hours.

### Step 3: Clean All Eight Cameras

FSD is entirely camera-based. No radar, no LiDAR — just eight cameras around the car. Dirty cameras degrade performance significantly.

Clean these camera locations with a microfiber cloth:
- **Front-facing**: center of windshield (interior, hard to reach — use a telescoping cloth)
- **Rear**: above the license plate
- **Side repeaters**: in the front fender mirrors (Model 3/Y) or B-pillars (Model S/X)
- **Pillars**: B-pillar and C-pillar cameras (usually clean but worth checking)

<div class="tip-box">
<strong>Don't skip the windshield interior:</strong> The front camera faces forward through the glass. A smudged windshield from the inside degrades performance more than exterior dirt. Use a glass cleaner and microfiber on the interior glass in the camera's field of view.
</div>

Check for chips or cracks in the windshield directly in front of the camera — FSD can be blocked or impaired by glass damage. See our [windshield and camera calibration guide](/posts/tesla-autopilot-camera-calibration/) if cameras need recalibration after cleaning.

### Step 4: Recalibrate Cameras If Needed

If you've had any windshield work done recently, or if Autopilot has felt slightly off, run a camera recalibration before FSD launches:

1. Tap **Controls > Service > Camera Calibration**
2. Drive on roads with clearly visible lane markings (highways are best)
3. Calibration completes after roughly 40 km of driving

### Step 5: Check Your 12V Battery

FSD relies heavily on the onboard computers. A weak 12V battery causes all kinds of ADAS misbehavior. If your 12V battery is more than 3 years old, or if you've had any warning messages about it, now is the time to check or replace it — before you're relying on FSD in city traffic.

See our [12V battery guide](/posts/tesla-12v-battery-replacement/) for diagnostics and replacement steps.

### Step 6: Resolve Any Open Alerts

FSD will not engage on a car with active service alerts or camera faults. Check your notifications panel and resolve anything flagged. Common blockers:

- Camera calibration required
- Forward camera blocked
- Autopilot degraded
- Service required

### Step 7: Check Your Driver Profile

FSD uses your driver profile to remember settings. Make sure your profile is set up correctly:

1. Tap your profile icon at the top of the screen
2. Confirm your preferred speed offset, follow distance, and autopilot settings
3. FSD will inherit these preferences

### Step 8: Budget for Month One

When FSD becomes available in your country, you'll see the option in **Controls > Autopilot > Full Self-Driving (Supervised)**. Subscription is month-to-month. Have a payment method confirmed in your Tesla account.

## Safety Expectations: Be Realistic

FSD v14 is meaningfully better than any Autopilot you've used in Europe. But set honest expectations for European launch:

- **Early builds will make mistakes.** European roads are diverse — narrow alleys in Italian towns, Dutch bicycle infrastructure, German autobahn merges without speed limits. The model has been trained, but no model is perfect at launch.
- **Weather affects it.** Heavy rain, snow obscuring lane markings, sun at low angles — these all reduce camera performance. FSD will disengage in conditions it can't handle safely.
- **You are the backup, always.** This is a Level 2 system. Your legal responsibility doesn't change because FSD is active.
- **Construction zones and temporary markings** are the most common disengagement trigger. On normal European roads, expect smooth operation. On roads with temporary lane setups, expect to take over.

The best mental model: FSD is a very capable trainee driver that you're supervising. It handles most situations correctly and improves constantly. You're there for the edge cases.

April 10 is the starting gun, not the finish line. Even if RDW approves on schedule, your car won't necessarily receive FSD that same day — Tesla typically stages OTA rollouts over days to weeks to manage server load and catch any issues early.

But the infrastructure will be in place. Check your hardware, clean your cameras, keep your software current, and you'll be among the first wave when the update arrives in your country.
